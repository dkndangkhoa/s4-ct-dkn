Tests for proofs of concept (both technical and with target group) üõù


With the laser order running late, we got creative and built a low-fi prototype using white string, chairs, and pins! We mapped the concept based on our sketch and 3D model.
Our user testing went great! The participants immediately grasped the goal of navigating the maze without tripping the wires. They seemed genuinely entertained by the experience ‚Äì there was a lot of laughter! Afterward, we explained our vision for the final product: a similar maze with real lasers, smoke effects, and immersive music. We even included a fun "alarm" sound when the laser is triggered. The feedback was overwhelmingly positive ‚Äì everyone said they'd love to see this installation come to life on the streets!



In fact, a few participants suggested some awesome additions:
Increased difficulty: A couple of people mentioned wanting a more challenging maze, maybe with branching paths or time limits.
Themed elements: Some suggested incorporating a specific theme into the maze design, like a sci-fi movie or a spaceship, to enhance the experience.



Applied Emotional Design by Don Norman üîó


We took inspiration from Don Norman's Emotional Design theory, focusing on the visceral level. We aimed to pique users' curiosity by making the maze visually appealing. We incorporated shapes that might evoke childhood nostalgia, encouraging interaction and a positive emotional response.



Sprint 3Ô∏è‚É£ Demo üßëüèª‚Äçüíª


We showcased our entire concept, starting with the planned technologies (like lasers, sensors and smoke machines]) then we ask our professor and stakeholders test out our Lo-Fi prototype, and they dug it! They gave some great feedback to take things to the next level. While they enjoyed the core gameplay, they suggested we beef up the narrative around it. Basically, small touches that can have a big impact without a ton of work. It's all about experimenting to see what enhances the experience. For example, maybe giving players a bandana with eye holes cut out to feel like a secret agent, or using a red light to make it clear when they trip a laser.
Some others great improvement suggestions emerged:
Testing the Real Deal: A key question was how we'd translate the concept to a real-world setting with functioning lasers and smoke effects. Users wanted to know how we'd set it up outdoors, generate excitement to attract participants, and potentially market the final product.
Final User Testing: There was also interest in our final user testing plan for the final sprint.
These are all fantastic points, and we're definitely considering how to address them moving forward. We'll need to brainstorm outdoor setup logistics, audience engagement strategies, and a potential marketing plan to get the word out. The final user testing plan for the real product is also crucial, and we'll be sure to have a solid approach in place.

Wrapping up Sprint 3, we held a retrospective with our project owner to dissect what worked well and deserves carry-over to the next sprint. We also identified areas for improvement, pinpointing things that might be slowing us down or hindering the effectiveness of user testing. Additionally, we brainstormed new ideas to enhance the implementation process for future testing sessions. This collaborative effort will ensure we're constantly iterating and optimizing our approach.


What went well:
Task devision and communication
What did not go well:
Due to a snag with the laser components, we weren't able to build the full prototype we initially envisioned. We had to use string to represent the lasers for user testing, which obviously limited the immersion factor. While the feedback was positive on the core gameplay (navigating the maze), it doesn't directly translate to the final product with real lasers and smoke effects.
What to do next:
Set up tent and test again with the real lasers and smoke machine as soon as we receive it.
